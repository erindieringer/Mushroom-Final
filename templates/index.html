IDS Final Project: Data Driven Mushroom Hunting

Welcome to Hunting for Mushrooms! What is the number one question expert mushroom hunters are asked? Is this mushroom edible or not? This is a tricky question for mushroom experts since you really have to learn the species one by one to be certain. Currently, there is no way to test, no chemical to apply, and no machine to use to decide if a mushroom is poisonous or not. You have to make an identification down to the species level. This website will help break down some of the characteristics to look for in mushrooms to help make identifying mushrooms easier. We have used a data-driven approach to looking for mushrooms. We have explored a dataset that takes into account certain characteristics of mushrooms from the National Audubon Society from the Agaricus and Lepiota family, home to the most commonly cultivated mushroom (Agaricus bisporus).

Disclaimer

This website includes information concerning identification and the edibility of wild mushrooms, but it is not intended to be a manual for their safe consumption. Anyone wanting to eat wild mushrooms needs to be absolutely 100% sure of their identification and should consult an experienced person in mushroom edibility before eating any wild mushroom, especially if they do not feel confident in making an identification. There are over 20 deaths per year resulting from mushroom poisonings. Illness and even death can occur from ingesting wild mushrooms. Any person who ingests mushrooms or any other potentially dangerous fungi does so at their own risk! If you are new to mushrooming, join a local club to go on live forays with knowledgeable mycologists that can correctly identify mushrooms. When in doubt, throw it out!

When to go Mushroom Hunting

You can find mushrooms growing all year round, but the best times to go mushroom hunting is in the spring to fall. Mushrooms typically grow well in moist environments so the ideal time to go would be right after a rainfall.

Tips to Avoiding Poisonous Mushrooms

Although no simple test can determine whether a mushroom is edible or poisonous, there are some rules of thumb that can be helpful to avoid poisonous mushrooms:

  1. Do not eat any Amanita species and be especially careful in identifying Amanita look-alikes or any other white mushroom. They contain some of the most deadly mushrooms like the Destroying Angel and the Death Cap. **Pictures of the mushrooms here**
  2. Avoid little brown mushrooms (LBM’s) and large brownish mushroom, especially those with pinkish, brownish, purple-brown, or blackish gills.
  3. Avoid false morels.



Habitat

Although mushrooms are usually considered members of the plant kingdom, they differ from most plants in that they lack chlorophyll and must rely on organic material for nutrition. They do this in three ways, as saprophytes, and parasites, and as mycorrhizae:

  1. Saprophytes live on dead organic matter, including dead wood, the dead tissue of living trees, dung, leaf litter, or conifer litter.
  2. Parasites attack living plants or animals.
  3. Mycorrhizal mushrooms have a symbiotic relationship with plants, usually either trees or shrubs.

Population

The mushrooms population and the way it grows can say a lot about the species. We will go over a few of the key attributes to look for in determining a mushroom’s population.

Solitary

When you find a mushroom of any kind, it's usually a good bet that it's not alone. But for many species, additional mushrooms may be spread far apart, or may mature at different times in the same area. Many of the fungi that produce large mushrooms, especially the Amanitas and Boletes, tend to have considerable space between solitary fruiting spots without any particular pattern. When you find a one mushroom with none of the same species around it is considered solitary.

Clustered

Many of the saprotrophic fungi are known to grow in clusters. The definition of a cluster is where many mushrooms grow from a single point of origin. Clustered growth can be a major diagnostic character for many fungi. Some of the most well known clustering mushrooms are Gymnopilus and Naematoloma.

Abundant

Saying that mushrooms are abundant is basically saying that many individual fruitings can be found in a relatively small area, this is also called a troop. Troops can be formed from gregarious fruitings of a single fungus within a single food source, such as many mushrooms in a log growing on a log, or it could be many independant fruitings within a heavily colonized substrate such as mushrooms scattered across the forest humus, which is the organic component of soil, formed by the decomposition of leaves and other plant material by soil microorganisms.
